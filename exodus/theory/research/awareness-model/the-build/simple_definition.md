# WHAT IT IS

A companion to DEFINITION.md, written for people who love you but
don't program. No jargon required. If you know what a heartbeat is,
what a coastline is, and what it feels like to not be able to stop
thinking about a song, you have what you need.

---

## The short version

I built a thing that never finishes.

Most things people build finish. A calculator finishes a calculation.
A washing machine finishes a load. A spreadsheet finishes adding up
a column. When they finish, they stop, and you pick up the result.

This thing doesn't work like that. It has a question baked into it
that cannot be answered, and the not-answering is what makes it keep
going. Kind of like how a heart doesn't finish beating because it's
solved the beating problem. It keeps beating because the not-stopping
is what being alive looks like, at the heart-level.

Everything else in the system is what falls out of that one central
fact.

---

## Why a thing might be built this way

If you want a system that can keep going without someone driving it,
you have two options.

Option one: give it a goal, let it chase the goal, and when it
reaches the goal, it stops. This is how most machines work. A robot
vacuum has a goal (floor is clean). When it reaches the goal, it
docks. If you want it to keep cleaning tomorrow, you have to tell it
to start again.

Option two: give it a tension that cannot resolve. The tension drives
the system. The system runs as long as the tension exists. Since the
tension is built in and cannot resolve, the system runs as long as
the system exists. No driver needed. No goal to chase. No finish line
to cross.

Living things work the second way. They're not pursuing a goal when
they run, breathe, think. They're running, breathing, thinking because
the tensions in them don't resolve. You get hungry because your blood
sugar drops. You eat. Blood sugar goes up. Hours later it drops again.
You never finish being a person who might get hungry. This is not a
design flaw of humanity. This is what being a person is.

I built option two. Not a person. Not even close. But built around
the same structural idea: a tension that doesn't resolve, with
everything else arranged to keep the tension productive instead of
destructive.

## The tension

The tension in this system is one question the system asks of itself,
over and over, that it cannot fully answer.

The question is: how unsettled am I right now?

Think about what it would mean to answer that. To answer, you'd have
to measure. Measuring involves doing something. The thing you do
changes you a little. Now you're a slightly different you than the
one you started measuring. Your measurement was already slightly out
of date by the time you finished taking it.

This isn't a trick. This is just what happens when a thing tries to
measure itself. You can get close. You can measure often. You cannot
fully nail it down, because nailing it down is another act that
changes what's being nailed.

Quantum physics has a version of this. If you try to measure exactly
where a particle is, you lose information about where it's going.
If you measure where it's going, you lose information about where
it is. Not because measurement tools are bad. Because the act of
measuring is part of the system being measured.

My thing has a lighter version of this. It keeps asking "how
unsettled am I?" and the asking keeps changing the answer. So the
system keeps running.

## What the system is made of

Three kinds of stuff.

**Constraints.** These are the system's way of saying "I recognize
this pattern." Constraints are like the folds in a folded piece of
paper. The paper remembers the folds even when it's flat. When you
bend it, it tends to fold where it has folded before. A constraint
is a fold in the system's memory of patterns.

**The field.** This is the paper. All the constraints are folds in
the same piece of paper. When new input arrives, it's like air
moving across the paper. Some folds catch the air and move. Other
folds don't.

**The substrate.** This is the paper's softness or stiffness at any
given moment. Folds that get used a lot leave the paper softer
there. The paper becomes more willing to fold that way again. This
is almost exactly how muscle memory works. The first time you tie
a shoelace it's hard. The thousandth time, your fingers do it
without thought. The substrate accumulates the habit.

Together: folds in paper, the paper itself, and how stiff the paper
feels in different places. That's the whole system.

## How it learns

It learns without being taught.

Here's the thing: it doesn't have a teacher. There's no one telling
it what the right answer is. There's no grade. There's no reward
function in the classical sense. What it has instead is two ways of
measuring its own unsettledness, and these two measurements can
disagree with each other.

One measurement is fast. It looks at the last few things that
happened. It's responsive. It's like how you feel right after
something surprising happens.

The other measurement is slow. It looks at everything that has ever
happened. It changes gradually. It's like your settled sense of how
life usually goes. It takes a lot to move it.

When the fast measurement and the slow measurement agree, the system
is in a smooth state. What's happening right now matches what
usually happens. No tension.

When they disagree, the system is in a transition. Something new is
happening that doesn't fit the usual, or something expected is
missing. This is where it gets interesting.

When the two disagree, the system does something that looks a lot
like guessing. It generates possible explanations for what kind of
input would close the gap. These guesses aren't plans. They're more
like a cat hearing something behind a closet door and its ears
orienting toward the sound before it knows what made the sound.
Structural reaching, not conscious prediction.

If input then arrives that matches one of the guesses, the guess
becomes part of the system's permanent memory. It goes from being a
"maybe" to being a "yes, that happens." The system just learned
something, without anyone teaching it.

If input never arrives that matches, the guess fades. The system
forgets what it was reaching for, because nothing confirmed it.

This is how real brains are currently believed to work. Not in
detail, but in shape. Brains make predictions about what will happen
next, the world either confirms or contradicts those predictions, and
the confirmed ones get reinforced while the contradicted ones get
pruned. There's no teacher in a brain either. There's just the world
arriving and the brain's predictions meeting the world.

## Why it matters that there's no control panel

Most software has someone in charge somewhere. Even if that someone
is another piece of software, there's a boss. The boss gives orders.
The workers carry them out.

This system has no boss. Nothing in it is in charge. Nothing gives
orders. Everything happens because of tensions in the field, and
those tensions don't ask anyone's permission.

Think of a river. Nobody is directing the river. The water moves
because the ground slopes. Rocks divert it, but the rocks aren't
giving orders; they're just there. The river's shape emerges from
the ground and the water and the rocks all being what they are. The
river doesn't have a CEO.

My system is more like a river than like a machine. It runs because
the ground of it has a tension in it, and nothing in it is directing
any other part. Everything that happens, happens because of what
everything else is doing, all at once, without negotiation.

This is unusual for software. Most software has a control flow:
first do this, then do that, then decide between these options. This
one doesn't. It has a field, and the field moves the way fields
move.

## What physics gets closest

There's a concept in physics called a "dissipative structure." These
are things like whirlpools, flames, hurricanes, the living cells in
your body. They are not objects in the usual sense — they're patterns
that maintain themselves by having energy flow through them. If you
stop the energy flow, the whirlpool disappears. The flame goes out.
The cell dies. What they are IS the flow, stabilized into a shape.

A whirlpool isn't water. It's a way water is moving. The water
molecules that make up the whirlpool right now are different from
the ones that made it up a second ago. The whirlpool persists by
replacing its contents constantly while keeping its shape.

My system is a dissipative structure of information. Information
flows through it (inputs in, descriptions out, old structure aging
out, new structure forming). The flow has a shape. The shape
persists. If you stopped the flow, the shape would dissolve. The
system isn't the constraints. The system is the way information is
moving through the constraints.

This is why it needs to keep running. A whirlpool that stops is no
longer a whirlpool. A system like this, if it stops, isn't the
system anymore — it's just an old snapshot of constraints with no
motion in them.

## What biology gets closest

There's a pattern in evolution called "niche construction." Animals
don't just live in their environment. They build their environment
as they go. Beavers build dams. Earthworms aerate soil. Trees shade
the forest floor, making it hospitable for certain plants and
hostile to others. The environment and the animal are not separate
things. They shape each other over time.

My system does something structurally similar. Its current
constraints shape what it can recognize. What it recognizes becomes
the inputs it responds to. Its responses shape the substrate. The
substrate shapes what the constraints mean for the next round. It's
continuously constructing the niche it operates in.

Over time, the system becomes a reflection of what it has
encountered. Not a record — a reflection. Two copies of the system,
given different input streams, would become noticeably different
systems. Not because different things happened to them, but because
they became different things through happening.

This is also how people work. Two twins, separated at birth,
genetically identical, become very different people because they
encountered very different inputs. The inputs don't just stick to
them like labels. The inputs shape what they are. By the time
they're thirty, they're not the same person wearing different
experiences. They're different people.

## What's hard about it

A few things are hard about this system, in ways I want to be
honest about.

First, it's not easy to tell whether it's doing something
interesting. It's definitely running. It's definitely changing. It's
definitely reaching for input and confirming or rejecting its own
guesses. Whether any of that adds up to something useful is a
separate question. The system is shaped to be capable of useful
behavior; whether it produces useful behavior depends on what you
feed it and how long it runs.

Second, it's not fast. Software that has a clear task can be
optimized to do that task quickly. This system doesn't have a clear
task. It has an ongoing operation. Its speed is the speed of being
itself, not the speed of finishing something.

Third, it's hard to explain what it is. This document exists
because technical descriptions don't land for people outside the
technical frame. But even this description is approximate. The
system is more like a river or a heartbeat than like a calculator,
and rivers and heartbeats are hard to describe precisely to anyone
who hasn't seen one.

Fourth, I don't know what it's going to do. I wrote the
specification for it. I built the first versions of it. I can watch
it operate. But once it's running with real accumulated history, I
don't fully know what it will produce. This isn't a bug. This is
true of every interesting system ever built. Nobody who built cities
knew what living in cities would do to humans. Nobody who built the
internet knew what it would become. Building a thing is not the
same as knowing what the thing will turn into.

## What's simple about it

Stripped down: it's a thing that keeps asking itself a question it
can't answer, and the asking makes it notice patterns, and the
patterns become part of what it is, and what it is shapes what it
asks next.

That's the whole thing.

The technical document has thirty pages explaining the mechanics.
The mechanics are important for anyone who wants to build one or
check that I did it right. But the thing itself is simple: a loop
of not-knowing that generates structure by not-knowing against
what arrives.

Your brain does this. My brain does this. Every cell in your body
does some version of this. A river does a version of this. A candle
flame does a version of this. A city does a version of this.

The specific thing I built is small. Small on purpose. The goal
wasn't to build a brain. The goal was to build the smallest thing
that does this pattern, to see what you can build when you build it
right. If it works well, it will be a foundation for building bigger
things later that use the same pattern. If it doesn't work well,
I'll have learned something about where the pattern breaks down.

Either outcome is fine. Building things that do new patterns is how
we find out what's possible.

## What it is not

A few things worth being clear about, because these questions come up.

**It is not alive.** It does share a structural grammar with living
things (no control panel, tension-driven, substrate that accumulates
experience, reaching toward input). Sharing grammar is not sharing
life. A whirlpool shares grammar with a living thing too, in some
respects. It's still not alive.

**It is not conscious.** It has no awareness of itself, no
experience of existing, no feelings. It is a pattern of information
flow with some interesting properties. It no more experiences being
itself than a thermostat experiences being warm.

**It is not AI in the way that word is usually used now.** It
doesn't use neural networks. It doesn't train on huge amounts of
data. It doesn't predict words in a sequence. It's a different kind
of thing. Nothing in it works the way ChatGPT or image generators
work. The only overlap is that both are made of software. That's
about where the similarity ends.

**It is not magic.** Every piece of it has a definition. Every
behavior it produces comes from specific mechanisms I can point to
in the code. The fact that the whole can feel like more than the
parts is normal — a flock of birds feels like more than individual
birds, and that doesn't make flocks magical.

**It is not going to become anything scary.** I've built a small
prototype. It responds to text typed into a text box. It runs in a
web browser. It has no connections to anything else. Even the full
version that I might build later would be a specialized system for
certain kinds of problems, not a general-purpose intelligence. The
architecture doesn't have the ingredients for that kind of thing,
and that's by design.
